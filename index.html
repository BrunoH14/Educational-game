Trace the Roots of Human rights

Contemporary human rights law has important historical predecessors. The value system that is manifested and expressed in human rights is neither a specifically Western one, nor an invention of the 20th century - the belief that every human being is born with certain rights, which allow him/her to live a life in dignity, is rooted in a variety of cultures, religions and early traditions worldwide. The idea of human rights evolved over a long period of time and was written down ("codified") in several major stages. We will look at some of the major steps in human rights evolution.

"One expression of the global value system that forms the basis of human rights is the "Golden Rule": Did you know that the "Golden Rule" can be traced in all major religions around the globe and is an important contribution to the idea of human rights?

"What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others." Confucius

Similarly, the African concept of "ubuntu" is based on the idea of what it means to be human, emphasizing respect for all members of the community as well as hospitality and generosity. "A person is a person through other people". If we are human through others, then dehumanizing another also dehumanizes us, which is why we need to promote and respect the rights of others.

For example: "No one of you is a believer until he desires for his brother that which he desires for himself." (Islam). "Do naught to others which, if done to thee, would cause thee pain: this is the sum of duty." (Hinduism). "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you." (Christianity). "What is hateful to you, do not to your fellow man. That is the entire law: all the rest is commentary." (Judaism). "Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful." (Buddhism).

Most societies had oral or written traditions similar to this "Golden Rule": for example, the Inca and Aztec codes of conduct and justice and an Iroquois Constitution were Native American sources that existed before the 18th century.

18th century BCE

Code of hammurabi

Babylonian King Hammurabi issues the first written law code, the Code of Hammurabi, based on revelation from Shamash, the god of justice, vowing to make justice reign in the kingdom.

6th century BCE

Charter of Freedom of Mankind

Cyrus the Great, King of the Medes and the Persians, issues the "Charter of Freedom of Mankind." It is often considered the first charter of human rights, including rights to liberty, security, religious tolerance, freedom of movement, freedom from slavery, and some social and economic rights.

6th century CE

Abolition of Torture

China’s Sui dynasty abolished torture in the 6th century for almost the same reasons as in Europe during the era of enlightenment more than 1,000 years later.

13th century CE

Civil liberties - Magna Carta

In 1215, English nobles and members of the clergy made the King of England agree to abide by the law by drawing up the Great Charter of liberties. However, the Magna Carta protected only the rights of the privileged (nobles) and is not, therefore, about human rights as such. It is still widely cited in defence of civil liberties because it represented a limitation of the king's power and recognition of (some) individuals' liberties and rights.

13th century CE

Charte du Mande, Charte de Kurukan Fuga

Based on the codification of oral traditions from West Africa, they uphold principles such as decentralization, environmental conservation, human rights and cultural diversity.

16th - 20th centuries

Struggle Against the Ruling Classes - Some Examples

16th century

Freedom of religion

Akbar, the Muslim Mogul of India, introduced secular ideas and freedom of religion during his rule. He supported religious tolerance and made it a duty to make sure that "no man should be interfered with on account of religion and anyone could go over to any religion that pleases him."

17th century

Freedom of Speech - Bill of Rights

English parliament passed a bill declaring that it would no longer tolerate royal interference in its affairs, forbidding the monarch to suspend the law without Parliament's consent, specifying free elections for members of Parliament and declaring that freedom of speech in Parliament was not to be questioned.

18th century

Individual Rights - Declaration of Independence

In 1776, most British colonies in North America proclaimed their independence from the British Empire in the United States Declaration of Independence, based on the "natural right" theories of Locke and Montesquieu, aiming to contain government power and protecting liberties - the Declaration advanced notions such as inalienable rights; the protection of individual rights; freedom of speech, press, petition and assembly; privacy; due process of law; equality before the law and freedom of religion.

18th century

Natural Rights - French Declaration on the Rights
of Man and Citizen

In 1789, the French people overthrew their monarchy and established the first French Republic. The Declaration attacked the political and legal system of the monarchy and defined the natural rights of man as "liberty, property, security and the right to resist oppression". It replaced the system of aristocratic privileges of the monarchy with the principle of equality before the law.

19th century

Right to vote for women

In 1893, New Zealand becomes the first nation to grant women the right to vote.

20th century

International Labor Organization

In 1919, the International Labor Organization (ILO) was established to oversee international treaties protecting workers with respect to their rights, including their health and safety.

20th century

Prohibition of Slavery

In 1926, the International Slavery Convention was adopted, intending to abolish slavery in all its forms.

World War II - present

Modern human rights development - A new world order

It took the catalyst of World War II to finally propel human rights onto the global stage and into the global conscience.

The atrocities committed in the course of this war - the Holocaust with its more than six million Jewish victims, the persecution of hundreds of thousands of Sinti, Romani, homosexuals, persons with disabilities and other groups who were opposed to the Nazi regime, as well and massive war crimes - finally brought worldwide attention to the need for a universal system for the guarantee and protection of human rights: Never again should anyone be unjustly denied life, freedom, food, shelter, and nationality - calls came from across the globe for human rights standards to protect citizens from abuses by their governments, standards against which states could be held accountable for the treatment of those living within their borders. Never again should anyone be unjustly denied life, freedom, food, shelter, and nationality.

Thus, World War II sparked the emergence of a further body of international law and, above all, the creation of human rights as we know them today: States committed themselves to establishing the United Nations, with the primary goal of bolstering international peace and preventing conflict, on a foundation of fundamental human rights, dignity and worth of the human person and on the equal rights between men and women. The UN Charter was adopted in 1945."

Foundation of the UN

1945: The United Nations (UN) is established as an intergovernmental organization to promote international co-operation and to prevent another conflict such as WWII - replacing its predecessor, the League of Nations. At its founding, the UN had 51 member states - currently there are 193.

First International War Crimes Trials

1945 - 1949 Nuremberg and Tokyo trials: Some of those responsible for crimes committed during World War II are brought to trial in Nuremberg and Tokyo; officials from the defeated countries are punished for committing war crimes, "crimes against peace," and "crimes against humanity." These trials are considered the start of the development of international criminal law.

1945 - 1950

1948: Representatives from the 51 member states of the UN come together under the guidance of US politician and activist Eleanor Roosevelt to devise a list of all the rights that everybody across the world should enjoy. This became the Universal Declaration of Human Rights - 30 rights and freedoms that belong to all of us. The rights they included continue to form the basis for all international human rights law.
